Matchbox

Project Lead | Apr 2024 - Aug 2025

Overview

Matchbox is a DIY STEAM (Science, Technology, Engineering, Art, Math) kit my friends and I developed for beginners to assemble their very own video game controllers made from upcycled keyboards and various 3D-printed parts! It's a fun and accessible introduction to hardware engineering!

People

Project Lead: Enoch Kang
PCB Engineer: Bryan Pong
CAD Engineer: Hei Hu
Guest Artist: Amanda Yingcharoen

Why

Problems:
  • Many STEAM toys are considered expensive in Asia, making them inaccessible for beginners as public schools are unlikely to have funding to afford getting each kid their own kit.
  • We generate around 40 million tons of e-waste every year. A large percentage of which consists of obsolete keyboards.
Solution(?):
  • Matchbox is an affordable DIY controller kit designed for beginners with parts sourced from obsolete keyboards.
  • By assembling a controller from inexpensive upcycled parts, everyone gets to have a fun and accessible introduction to hardware engineering!

How

Affordable Hardware

The Matchbox kit is made from upcycled components and common electrical components like breadboards and push buttons. This helps keep the cost down while still providing a fun and engaging learning opportunity!

Accessible Workshops

The second crucial component of the Matchbox project is how the workshops are designed. We spent months iterating on exactly how the workshop is delivered so that we can make sure learners of any skill level will be able to complete their very own working Matchbox by the end of a 2hr workshop.
This promise is important to us because I believe beginner level STEAM education material can be a double edge sword: when done well, the experience could inspire the learner to begin pursuing their curiosity in STEAM concepts; but when done poorly, a bad first experience with STEAM can convince a beginner that they are not an "engineering person" and miss out on many wonderful creative opportunities for the rest of their lives.

Adaptive Software

Our custom workshop software was created to solve two problems:
  • Each learner has a different amount of prior experience and wants to move at a different pace. Yet the workshop staff can only advance at one pace (often the slowest one to accommodate newer people).
  • Assembly is complicated and many steps are difficult to backtrack after some point. If we only help the learners test their controllers at the end of the workshops there will be a high likelihood they made a mistake earlier, didn't realize it, and now the error has snowballed into something unsolvable within 2hrs (fails the 2hr workshop completion promise).
We iterated on many versions of the workshop material before settling on building our own digital instruction manual tool. Our tool features step by step pictures of the assembly, alongside an always-available keyboard tester tool at the bottom of the screen. This tool is important because we want the kids to develop the habit of testing as they go, and we can install checkpoints in each major step of the assembly so that we can troubleshoot early and not have to backtrack too much under a time constraint.
The digital instructions also provided the learner with all the material to follow at their own pace. We found this was especially effective in solving the pacing issue when the range of experience is large in a group. The experienced learners can peek ahead and won't become bored and disengaged while waiting, and the less experienced can take their time knowing that they will still have access to everything (the manual is just a website) even if they cannot finish all the steps during the workshop time.

Development

(This is a summary of our development journey. You can read the full version here!)

Inspiration (v0) | Aug 2019

The first Matchboxes were created in 2019. Inspired by the MakeyMakey, I wanted to make Scratch-compatible controllers to play my Scratch games. The prototype simply has 10 buttons per controller connected to a MakeyMakey sitting inside a plastic box.

v0.1 Prototype | Apr 2024

The first prototypes using actual keyboard parts was the v0.1. We started by simply soldering each pin on the keyboard PCB directly to the blank PCB board to expose the pins to the top. Then we could wire buttons to simulate keyboard button presses.
After the internals were working, Hei made the first Matchbox shells. This allowed us to hide all the messy wires and fragile connections in a box and have proper button caps! We also experimented with different button switches. The black controller has clicky buttons while the white one has soft membrane buttons. Models were made in Fusion360 and printed at our university’s makerspace (Innowing @ HKU)

v0.2 Prototype | May 2024

There were 2 main problems with v0.1:
  • Too many wires
  • Hand-soldering takes too long!
So we focused on solving those problems in v0.2. Bryan got to work putting together a custom PCB (Printed Circuit Board) that could replace our old hand-wired version.
We didn’t have any experience making PCBs at the time so Bryan asked his friend Austin to help us out. As you can see from the comparison below, the custom PCB greatly reduces the complexity!
After we got the PCB working somewhat consistently, we had the chance to demo at our university's InnoShow (think Maker Faire). We got the attention of a few important professors and got the first boost we needed to really start testing and developing the project more seriously. The first step in this new stage was of course, workshop!

1st Workshop | Jun 2024

On June 8th, 2024, we had our very first Matchbox Workshop! We invited around 10 of our friends to come and assemble their very own Matchbox!
As with the first time doing anything, many things went wrong during the workshop. After hearing feedback and reflecting, we mapped out all the problems and potential solutions on our Miro board.

v0.3 Prototype | Aug 2024

We searched far and wide to find a better connection method for v0.3. We even tried tensioning each pin individually with tiny springs the size of velcro hooks! Imagine soldering a whole row of 30 pins by hand!
Eventually, we learned about elastomeric connectors and realized they would be perfect for the job. The team quickly put together a new prototype to test out our idea. After around a week, the PCBs came back and we eagerly put together the controller...
And it actually worked pretty well! But most importantly - no more soldering was required! Here's a pic of Hei (left) & Bryan (right) playing a celebratory round of Lego Star Wars with the v0.3 Matchboxes when we finally got it working!

v0.4 Prototype | Mar 2025

After more workshops and iteration, we are currently at prototype v0.4! This iteration focuses on simplifying the workshop assembly experience while retaining the customizability for advanced users to hack and make whatever they want (lowering the floors while keeping the ceilings high, if you will). Here are the key improvements:
  • Alignment and orientation: The new "TOP SIDE" label and triangle cut out of the corner ensure it's (nearly) impossible to assemble the PCB upside down or inside out (people somehow found ways to do both - at the same time! - with our previous version).
  • Pre-mapped wiring options: The new presets of keys at the top of the PCB help people map and remap their Matchboxes easily without the complicated lookup table (v0.3). This saves valuable time in the workshops and makes more time for playing games!

The "A" in "STEAM"

Art spotlight!
The Matchbox project wouldn't have become what it is without our wonderful friends and guest artist collaborators! Although art does not directly influence the Matchbox hardware or workshops, design helps us define the Matchbox's identity and make it more than just an assortment of plastic and electronic parts - it becomes a spark with a story to tell - a spark of curiosity!
The iconic Matchbox box art was designed by Amanda Yingcharoen. My contribution was suggesting the moth and the idiom “星火燎原”, which roughly translates to:
"A small spark is enough to start a blazing fire."
The idiom echoes our mission for Matchbox to become the first spark of inspiration to light the fire of passion for more beginners!
Amanda had the idea to make the Matchbox box look like a real matchbox! We looked at retro matchbox designs and also took a lot of inspiration from Art Nouveau artists we liked.
Continuing that chain of thought, a staff from our makerspace saw the Matchbox boxes and remarked that it would be really cool if there was actually some match-shaped thing in the kit. At the time, we were thinking of including screwdrivers in the sets since we didn't want to assume a beginner had one and also because I was inspired by this story from Tony Fadell about screwdrivers:
"We knew it (the screwdriver) wasn’t just a hardware tool—it was a marketing tool. It helped customers remember Nest. It helped them fall in love." - Tony Fadell, Build: An Unorthodox Guide to Making Things Worth Making
The screwdriver is such an iconic tool - to have the opportunity to be the one responsible for giving a child their first screwdriver and helping them believe that they could be engineers, oh what an honor! If we were going to include a screwdriver, I knew it couldn't be a regular screwdriver... It had to be something a child could cherish. It should be a Matchstick Screwdriver!
